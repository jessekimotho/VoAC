<a href="http://voiceofanafricanchild.files.wordpress.com/2017/02/angry-man-1409746880rai2.jpg"><img src="http://voiceofanafricanchild.files.wordpress.com/2017/02/angry-man-1409746880rai2.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-24 alignnone size-full" width="1920" height="1271"></a>​Matatu-mates, to us guys are a life goal. The pretty girls we meet in <i>matatus </i>are the second reason we use perfume. (the first being we probably didn't shower that day) Not that we engage them in conversations anyway. No. We just love that feeling of sitting next to those beautiful girls we&nbsp;only interact with on social media.&nbsp;

That lightskin who posted on Facebook about her instagram account "Follow for follow". You rush to insta and find she follows a mere 200 out of her 15K followers. Either the school you attended got the meaning of the second "follow" wrong or your eyes are messing. Certainly not that this damsel is lying. Is she? No. Hell no! So you just follow her and wait to be "followed back". Does she? Of course she does. Why wouldn't she? You are, after all the son of <i>Mzee Murage </i>who sits at the council of kiama in every <i>ruracio </i>in the village and you boast 321 followers out of a folowing of 1000+. So, yes she will follow you back.

But now you're together in a mat.(what is <i>sako kwa bako </i>in English btw?) Now she will notice you by fire by force. She has to know even you "Nairobi you arrived." She needs to know you also use that large infinix she ignores your DMs with-only you don't have the pink cover. She must know that when she pulls out fifty bob from her huge handbag to pay the fare, you shall take it, put it in your pockets and pay for <i>wawili </i>using a one hundred shilling note because your father also sold a cow so that you go to school and learn maths. You shall act like nothing happened because you're awesome like that. You shall fake a call and speak fluent English because your <i>teasher </i>was Mr. Kimani with the motorbike.&nbsp;

You will fake an argument with an imaginary "client" and act cool about it because that is the kind of man she and her Guarana-addict friends have as "goals". You shall ignore the tattoo on her left thigh. The one of an eagle with a bleeding eye, broken talons, rugged feather, her initials <i>GMA</i> written in italicized bold and whose artist probably shook because the wings are slightly assymetrical...okay yeah. You get the drift. DON'T ignore that one.&nbsp;

Generally, there's always that feeling that you have <i>"</i>arrived" when you enter a <i>mat </i>and find an empty seat next to a girl with real eyebrows. With strong, classy perfume reminding you that there's hope for Nairobi, global warming notwithstanding. It is always a good sign. Don't argue with science. And if it so happens that she skips all seats and voluntarily chooses the one next to you-especially if the bus is empty-my friend, it's time to go home. You've had too many a bottle. These things don't happen.&nbsp;

So on this particular day I dared to dream. I have already established Thursdays to be my lucky day. Back since my high school days. I mean, wasn't it the day when we would finally withdraw our money from the bursar's office at school? Were we not usually served sausages in the morning for breakfast? Wasn't supper normally either fish or chicken? Not to mention it was the day I left school for good.&nbsp;

I wasn't taking any more chances with my Thursdays. The other Thursday I'd accidentally met my crush (pronounced girlfriend) in town wearing the same shirt she'd seen me in for three consecutive times. Why do these things happen though? So that day I was going to meet her, in a different shirt. A white one. A plain white shirt in case you didn't hear the first time. I had showered 90% for her, 9% because it is the right thing to do and -99% because it was January and there was no use wasting water trying to look clean for people. So yes, that maths adds up to I showered 0%.

I sat there oozing with the masculine scent of my designer perfume plastered on layers of dirt. I patiently waited for maiden in glowing gown to waltz into the seat next to mine. We would then have a quiet, pleasant drive to wherever I was going. This would later be narrated to my boys as how I swept a stranger girl of her &nbsp;matatu seat using my smooth vibes. So much that she refused to give me her number to "protect her relationship." I had a pair of white shorts. Again, I say, plain white shorts.

I had bought Delamere Vanilla yoghurt like I always do when travelling. Not cool, I know. But if I were to choose between Vanilla yoghurt and your validation of my coolness, I'd ask you to rate my hotness on a scale of you to me. (Pronounced 0-10)

Either my Thursday fàiry had missed the bus or today wasn't precisely a Thursday in the fairy calendar. There's this lady who entered the bus, carrying like four large bags with a baby tugging at her jeans screaming, "Mummy nataka ileee..." Right after her, enters, ladies and gentlemen, my preferred <i>matatu-mate </i>for the day. The lady with the still screaming baby struggles into the bus and places her bags onto the seat directly opposite me. Phew!

Not that I have a problem with sitting next to mothers with small babies. I mean, who doesn't like making faces at them and acting all serious when they start crying. Like we have no idea why. I just preferred instagram-beauty league infinix user at that moment. On the real though, she had an infinix. That large one with a fingerprint sensor beneath the camera. &nbsp;

What happened next happened so fast, I still try to &nbsp;discover how I had no time to say <i>"nimeshikia mtu hapa."</i>&nbsp;I was busy texting Bae that <i>nimefika nyayo, jam imeshikana. I'm coming. </i>Never mind I'd just boarded a <i>manyanga </i>at Galleria.

The baby pointed at me. Well, at my yoghurt but no, I didn't see that. It was January and not even innocent baby eyes were going to convince me to part with the product of my hard-earned 100bob. <i>Pesa otas </i>my friend.

<i>"Unataka kukaa hapo?" </i>Silence.

<i>"Utakaa hapo mummy?</i>" Silence Sn 2.

<i>"Sawa basi, tukae hapa na uncle, sindio kamum?"</i>&nbsp;

Wait, I'm sorry, what now??!! In what language does two silences make a yes again? I tried making faces at the baby to coerce her to change her mother's mind but if only my face had been on that yoghurt pack.&nbsp;

Mother placed one bag down, sat baby on her lap and squeezed one of the plastic bags between us. I mean, she just sat. No slow motion, no sad background music, no apologies for killing a dream. She just sat.&nbsp;

I offered to help with the baby as the engine roared to life. She had her hands full,for one, and most importantly, there was absolutely no way I was placing those bags anywhere near my all white clad.&nbsp;

<i>"Enda ushikwe na </i>uncle<i>"</i>

Oh lord no!

<i>"Na </i>uncle?<i>"</i>

<i>"Eeh, na uncle"</i>

If you think finding the perfect balance between the taste of ugali and <i>sukuma wiki </i>in your mouth is hard,then you definitely haven't tried visually communicating to a three-year-old Nairobi girl you just met that your relationship is still too young to be sharing yoghurt already.

"Uncle <i>nataka ii," </i>she said, pointing at my yoghurt.

My friend if you have not mastered the art of biting lemon by the time you're leaving your village for the city, don't come.&nbsp;

See why I don't like titles? The way I heard iñ Uganda once you acquire the title "husband" you automatically owe someone's daughter a house. Now this young girl thinks I owe her my yoghurt because I'm her de facto "uncle." Poor girl also probably thinks I'd reserved the seat her mum is on for them. Lol! She has no idea.

The little girl, now my apparent niece (I'll be having a word with my brother over this issue. I wouldn't be surprised if I was actually the uncle though) threw a mini tantrum over my yoghurt as her mother sat there, in silence, scrolling through her Facebook feed. I mean, people!! What happened to some good ol' Whooping oñ the behind. I remember at, almost that age, my mother gave me a thrashing at the supermarket tills. True story!
<blockquote>See there's this Asian boy who had picked a "jawbreaker" from the shelves. He unwrapped it and stuck the huge sweet in his mouth. He then took the wrapper to the till where her mum was and she paid for it. #ThugLife, right? After careful observation, this seemed like a cool enough idea to try at my age.

So I'm here busy trying to unwrap jawbreaker. Small small, I catch the scent of my mum's perfume and it finally hits me we don't share a mum with the Asian boy who, by now, is in the car being praised for being responsible.

I had no time to react. My cheeks were in between her index fingers and thumbs on both sides. She pulled me so hard I was almost suspended in the air. Then out of nowhere the release of my cheeks elastically catapulted two rap tap slaps straight in my face.

Those days nobody used to <i>tetea </i>you when you were being beaten. No. People would go on shopping as if a baby sprawling on the floor screaming for help from its own mother was a shopping experience the supermarket owed them.&nbsp;

The only thing that was getting me out of this situation was if I climbed the Nakumatt elephant and rode it all the way back to Thailand.

In retrospect, I can't exactly tell how that beating added any value to my life but I would sure as hell do the same to my daughter. Right after naming her after my mother, &nbsp;of course and screaming to her,"Susan <i>ni aibu gani unaniletea mbele ya watu, eeh?"</i>&nbsp;Sweet revenge, right? Now that I think of it, who was bringing who shame?</blockquote>
I tightly shut the lid of my yoghurt and gave it to her. Knock yourself out! This is Kenya my friend. Things don't come easy. Four unsuccessful times later, she hurled the tetra &nbsp;paçk at me, missing the danger zone by inches. At this, her mother took her from me, fearing-rightfully so-that I was&nbsp;about to toss the little bundle of misery off the window, after she had earlier, also deliberately stepped on the hem of my shorts.MY.WHITE.SHORTS!!

She pinched me and slapped me on the lap for the third time in a row as her mother smiled at me sheepishly. Then she finally broke the silence, <i>"Huyu akiwa na njaa anakuanga hivi. Wacha kupiga uncle kamum." </i>Like hell, yeah I wanted to know more about her, thank you. Is she seeing anyone, I'd like to know that too. The conductor beckoned at me for our fare as stranger mother shoved stranger baby crazy into my face to retrieve her purse. And damn no lil' biih. This guy right here isn't your "uncle"

I gave the conductor 200bob and he asked me <i>"wangapi?". </i>I said <i>mmoja </i>like a boss and he gave me 100bob and motioned at the lady to hand me the 50bob she had. The baby grabbed my now 50 shilling note and said, <i>"Leta pesa ya mamangu." </i>Her mother looked at her adoringly and smiled. I wondered what was going on in her head.<i>(aaaww...I'm bringing up a thief who's right about now being seriously flogged in someone's mind)</i>

Beep...Beep...<i>umefika wapi?</i>&nbsp;It was Bae. Oh no! Remember I was "in Nyayo" 30 minutes ago, right? I was in Nyayo for reals now. I didn't know whether to text her <i>tumepotea njia </i>or what.&nbsp;And oh was there traffic in Nyayo!!

I decided to alight and walk up past the last round about to tao where traffic is normally fluid. Baby crazy &nbsp;was asleep by now.

"Excuse me" I pleaded.

"Unashuka?"

Me:"Yeah"

Inner me:"Hapanaa, naenda washrooms huko nyuma ya basi."

She tilted her laps sideways as I squeezed my way out from my seat.

I pulled my &nbsp;fifty-shilling note from the surprisingly firm grip and she immediately woke up in a mouthful of wails. Cry or cry not, if they thought I was going to pay their fare after the pride I'd invested in saying <i>Mmoja, </i>they had clearly over-estimated my social status with reference to my fashion taste.

I stepped off the bus in a virtual slow motion as the crying baby's noises made for the background music. The breeze blew into my face as I took in a luñgful of cool, fresh air, finally free of imposed responsibilities. I turned to make a face at the girl but my phone buzzed midway.&nbsp;

Oh dammit!! <i>I have a date to get to and a lie to come up with!! </i>Run brother, run!
