Annus horribilis: - That is the best term to describe last year.

2016 was messed up for most of us, yes? We lost soldiers in Somalia, we got mad when we discovered that SGR trains look like old tractors, 5 billion from the health ministry went missing, teargas Monday destroyed businesses and if you were in form four last year, CS Matiang’i made sure you didn’t get an A. Worse still, when we thought the problems just existed in Kenya; Donald Trump was declared president elect; simply annus horrribilis. Even Kofi Olomide can tell you the year was horrible… but I digress, this post is not about all that.

Today we’ll talk about the problems no one talks about; nothing generic,nothing newsworthy or even blog worthy. Today we open up and talk about the E that stared at you with a menacing look last sem, the rejections that came your way, the horrible relationship you developed with your dad. The audition that you rehearsed over and over again and did not work out, the interview that you slouched. Just for this Friday, allow me to remind you of the heart break you got last year.Let us open up to the first sexual encounter that you thought would be great and well you just realized it was a booty call, yeah?

<strong><i>Class 7s- Mathare Primary S</i><i style="line-height:1.7;">chool.</i></strong>

I presumably carried some of these frustrations last year when I became a teacher at Mathare slums. Then I started noticing something weird. I had an English lesson with class 7s every Wednesday at 3:30pm. I would try to make them write great compositions, but of course it was tough. These are slum kids and English is almost foreign to them. To easen up the task, I made them narrate to me the stories they wanted to write and slowly I would teach them how to construct meaningful paragraphs. But pretty much, these kids taught me more about life than I could ever teach them.

They told me dismaying stories of how they are forced into child trafficking and into selling drugs. The girls told me how they were introduced to prostitution by their own mothers, they told me how they slept hungry or how their father came home drunk and forced them to sleep outside. Sadly, they even told me of how their boyfriends bought them red undies on valentines and later forced their way through them.These kids told me stories that I felt were of disgrace and later those kids wrote the best stories on drug and substance abuse, they wrote the most beautiful stories on talent, on overcoming fear, about their dreams. None of those kids was sad, they were always jovial and looked forward to Wednesday lessons and so did I.Those kids have tenacity and the will to live. They know what life truly is and they live it; whether it makes them happy or not. They are so used to their sad stories that they are no longer sad; they are stories they want to tell when they grow up.

<em><strong>Vulnerability</strong></em>.

You see the thing with our problems is that we do not talk about them. We do not talk because we don’t want anyone regarding us as weak; we fear being thought of as weak.  We don’t talk because we don’t want to be a burden to others. We don’t want anyone to think we cannot make it, so we put on this tough façade. And what do we do after that? We become better people? No. I’ll tell you what we do; we build walls and drown ourselves in depression, we drink ourselves silly till we are wretched souls, we bake weed all day and get lit and then you know what next? We become comfortable with that life.

You disagree? Then tell me why is it that when you ask someone of their love story; they end up telling you a tale of a horrifying heartbreak? Why is it that the stories of connection end up being stories of disconnect? Yet, at the same time, why do a million stories of success have a bearing on suffering? Maybe; that single entity that defines someone as weak is actually the only other way to define them as strong. Maybe, that thin line between weakness and strength is being vulnerable to our own stories. Perhaps like Steve Jobs, remembering that we'll be dead soon is the most important tool we need to make the big choices in life. Because almost everything - all external expectations, all pride, all fear of embarrassment or failure - these things just fall away in the face of death, leaving only what is truly important.

Am I saying you should go telling everyone about your problems? By all means, no.I am just saying the next time someone asks you if you are fine and you really are not, tell them. The next time someone is willing to listen to you; do not push them away.The next time; just before you conclude that nothing ever works out in life ,reach out. You might just be surprised at how much similar crap we all go through and maybe just maybe the only difference is how we turn out.

Owning our stories can be hard but not nearly as difficult as spending our lives running from it. Embracing our vulnerabilities is risky but not nearly as dangerous as giving up on love and belonging and joy—the experiences that make us the most vulnerable. Only when we are brave enough to explore the darkness will we discover the infinite power of our light. ~Brené Brown
