I knew a buddy back in my previous life, High School you'd call it. For security purposes we shall call this guy Evans, because that is actually his real name. Now, young me fresh from the Kingdom of bananas had minute problems in conversation. I would end my conversations with the term, <em>"aajee?</em>!" of course in the obvious rising Kisii intonation. It would come out as an exclamation and a question at the same time. Call it the mother paradox of all intonations. So people would stay there waiting for me to either signal them for an expected answer, or continue with my mostly mundane folkores.

<em>Nikamwambia aajee?!!.......nikamtusi aajee?!!</em>
<em>Nikagusa huyo msichana halafu akageuka aajee?!!...</em>

Evans is the one who made me realise I had this intonation. He would tease me everytime. Which mostly pissed me off. But then I would tell him to pronounce the word contented. Hailing from the vast Eastern region of Kenya, the guy would bite his tongue off to small pieces before he finished the damn word. He would say condended. I swear by Sakawa the Great,that used to make me happy. Then I would forget my <em>aajee</em>?! and say it sometimes. And we start all over again.

You see, the kids in my highschool couldn't give you peace if the smallest accent was traced in your conversation. There were small grammar cartels,probably distant relatives of Shakespeare. So dudes of our likes straight from <em>shagz</em> would only talk to ourselves and in hushed tones.I swear I'm not lying. They even started a notice board at the back of the class permanently with a list of  people who mixed up <em>matamchi yao</em>. Hell, someone even had a notebook specifically for this stuff. You are there in class eating story then you say you were in a <em>baraimary</em>( primary) school somewhere in Mandera. The whole class goes oooooooooh and the cartel is chewing your buttocks off. <em>Unawekwa bahari yake ,aye?</em>

Mundane stuff I'm telling you. But it got serious .This was outright bullying. <em>Shagz</em> dudes we had to adopt or lose this pronunciation battles. So we retreated to ourselves and organised tribal groups where no one notices that <em>umeng'oa</em> because they either don'know or are also busy mispronouncing their own words. These in turn became the bookworms who would top class since time for conversations was spent on books. I'm not saying I was one of them, but I'm not saying I wasn't . <em>Yaani ninasema aajee?</em>!..heck, forget it.

I don't know if they amuse or amaze me, but my tribesmen are a funny crew. If you know one of them you've probably heard the word <em>gaki</em> in their sentences. They just throw the Kisii terms in there aimlessly like flying faeces in a paper bag. And they are very brave. They don't give a rat's ass If you notice their <em>matamshi</em>. Terrorists of language. Very brave they should be all working for KDF and plundering English and Swahili and the border, away from the serene of hardworking Kenyans.I have been a client of this conversation slaughtering. My word being ,<em>obe!</em> , an exclaimer mostly, It was curtailed by highschool grammar cartels. But I eventually joined the Nazis  after perfecting my own grammar.
They say if you cant beat the donkeys, join them. I don't  know who they are.

So having finally gone to the big city and read books, I knew my home crush would finally fall for me. So I did what every <em>Mogaka</em> doesn't do to a <em>Moraa</em>, I approached her. We hit the inner murram road with the sugarcane I had bought her. The inner road is where village women can't see us easily. They are very mouthy and might cook up stories. And also to avoid the local boys who might beat me because they think I am finishing for them options for girls. Ha. Fancy that. They will just beat me in front of my crush. Ugly nutheads.

Anyway, She was there biting that sugarcane very sexily and that really gets to me. It is a mini date this one. Me in a date with my crush. Even if I die now, I have made it in life. Then the way she chewed the juice out of it melted my heart. I was in love. Hah. Me in love. Even my mother would laugh at that. Good thing she can't read this post. So we are there and she has her white handkerchief and bata rubber shoes. I don't know what it is with Kisii chics and white handkerchiefs on their hands. So anyway again, we are walking and I am talking and I pause from time to time allowing her to bite my picnic sugarcane. I have invested in her. Today I might get a tumble. The dryspell I have is climate change I tell you; global warming.I am there throwing a few Nairobi sheng jargons while sprinkling a bit Kisii not to lose her totally. She does smile.
"Where do you go to church?"

I ask her as I try to rack my brains for a better vibe. As I wait for her reply I am thinking of my next lines. These flirts don't lay themselves son.

"Sent Balls Catholic."

"St. where now???" With a puzzled look . Few things rarely puzzle me. I am a man of constant face.

"St. Balls, <em>kwani husikii poa?</em>" She replies. She doesn't use my rare puzzled face as clue that I want to hear Paul's name in her sentence, not her weird fetish church. I put my face back in the chambers. I had wasted it. Then I break into laughter and she doesn't like it. I can't explain to her because she thinks she is right. And women are always right. So  we argued and I lost her.

All I want to say bottomline is simple here, since I can't go on writing forever. It doesn't matter how you pronounce words in English or Swahili as long as you know you can write them well and can still with your accent make yourself well understood. Whether you lace them with some local condend like my Kamba friend or say <em>pananas</em> like my folks, just live on. Kalembe Ndile has apparently studied abroad but you'd think he went to <em>Kithakaine</em> Polytechnic .But again we should laugh at each other. It is healthy as long as we don't do it too much,because some of the pronunciations are utterly funny.

Like a Somali friend of mine who will write a <em>boem</em> some day for us.
