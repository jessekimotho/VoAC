Dragging my bag behind me I find the door closed. I am ravening and gaunt from a day’s donkey work at school. Mama is not home. My small sister and her annoying little head are not around. By the way I don’t like her <em>kasmall</em> head because I am cursed with this bundle above my shoulder. Trust me; I get tired of my head sometimes. Especially when I am climbing a mountain then I feel like unplugging it from my shoulders and carrying it in my bag. Don’t laugh. It’s not even funny. It’s terrible. And babies don’t like big heads. You smile at one in a <em>matatu</em> and it shrieks. It’s a sad feeling. It has made me subject of all <em>mchongoano</em> at school. From being named after a famous bus called <em>Pacific</em> to the unwelcomed nickname of Headmaster, I have brought laughter to them who ridiculed me. Anyway back to this tired evening when I, the Headmaster and chief strategiest of <em>Pacific</em> travels hears sound of deep music from a homestead three farms away from our homestead. I decide to check the place out and see whether I can find my folks there.

Getting the digs, it’s like Jesus turned water into wine. The whole village is here. It’s like <em>Tomorrowland</em> or <em>Blankets</em> <em>and</em> <em>Wine</em>, only a village one. Also the wine is <em>busaa</em>. Music is local, just the way they like it. Hips are gyrating everywhere. I am in primary school but the amounts of huge behinds I have seen surpass the accent in a Nairobi lady standing outside Imax Cinema.

“Whats happening here?” I ask <em>Omboga</em> as I pull him away from the crowd of small boys eating a plate of plain white rice. Omboga is the one we got circumcised together with last year. He is named after some ugly vegetables and he doesn’t go to school. I don’t have reason for either name or illiteracy.

“<em>Omotu</em> has come out”, he says. Then it hits me, <em>Omotu</em> had gone under the knife two weeks before and is being released today. They call it to come out here. Someone taps my shoulder and a plate of rice is shoved on my chest as I struggle to hold it. It’s my mother and she is the one serving people in this event.

“Here, eat and look for your sister and take her home,” She says a she swings around, some of the words going with her. By the time I look up from my humongous plate content I can only see the bottom of her <em>lesso</em> a she goes into the kitchen. It reads,

<em>“Ulijifunza ngumi juzi wataka kupigana na Tyson?” </em>

I don’t know why people are afraid of Tyson. He is our ugly neighbor and I don’t think he has that much strength. I don’t like him. I don’t like that mama has a <em>lesso</em> with his name. Even my old man had issues with the name but mama said it’s about a certain Tyson who beats people for money in the US and is a legend. It’s very funny how people make money and name in the US. Maybe when I grow up I can beat Tyson our neighbor and mama will change her <em>lesso</em>.

It’s late in the night when I finish my plate and look for my sister. But first, I have to drink. Its dark and adults are drunk so they won’t notice. The <em>busaa</em> mug is in the house. At the very end of the small room is the Holy Grail; the mug of <em>busaa</em> that I had been looking for. I push my way between thighs of dancing men and women and brush my face past rugged beards and bear through terrible smell of sweat. This place has a pungent smell that makes me want to puke. An old woman sees me and hugs me. Gosh! I am going to suffocate in her embrace. She smells like witchcraft would smell on Christmas-eve. Ooh <em>Sakawa</em> save me! I am going to puke. When she finally let's go off me, the Holy grail is gone."

I find my sister and walk home dejected and disappointed. She’s blabbering something that sounds like monkeys eating bananas while making noise. I don’t listen because I don’t care.

I’m deep this dream where I am a prince surrounded by semi-naked women blowing air with leaves and feeding me small banana fruits when I wake up. In this life I am afraid of two things, darkness and sweatpants .Sweatpants because you never know when and where your manhood decides to misbehave. Darkness because well, Witches out there mehn. But now I have my stomach rumbling and I care less. I open the door and rush to the toilet outside. <em>Sema shock</em>! I know you didn’t say but I don’t like you anyway. So I don’t care.  I find people queued up holding their asses while doing that funny dance when you are pressed. The whole village is awake. Those who can’t hold it are in bushes. No one is safe. Men and women are out here, children are crying. My diarrhea can’t wait so I get a <em>Jembe</em> and dig myself a small hole for a temporary relieving.

The worst thing about diarrhea? When you can’t predict it. You squat there for five minutes and think you’re done and try standing up and it comes back and down you go. It’s a game of standing and squatting; almost like you’re doing a rugby <em>haka</em>. I discovered that despite diarrhea being tough there is a sweet feeling to relieving yourself.

The next day a village meeting was called and the owner of the festival admitted to have used yeast. Yeast on rice goddamit! People are out there debating whether to use pineapple on pizza and you’re adding yeast to rice. <em>Ati</em> to make it bloat and become a lot.  But not many people attended the meeting. I was remarkably absent because I having a shitty day. If you know what I mean
