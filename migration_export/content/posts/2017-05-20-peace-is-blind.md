She sits by the way to the church; right outside the gate. I see her, everybody does. With a small tin cup on her feeble quivering right hand, she shakes it continuously as if ascertaining its contents are intact.
"Why don't you ever come inside?" I engage her.
"In church? No. I cannot. God is in there," she replies still maintaining the shaking action. I pity her.
"Don't pity me. I am past that phase," she pauses and faces my side as if trying to read my mind.
"What's wrong with God being inside the church?" I ask her.
"Because I am angry with him. Because if I go there and face him, my anger might overcome my rationality and it will take myriad angels to save the spit. God allowed them to make me blind. He was himself blind when they committed atrocities I cannot adequately project."
"Is it okay if you tell me how all this came to happen?" I request.
"Tell me Okenye, is war the cost of peace? Do we have to go through baptism by fire for people to love each other in this nation?" She turns around to face me. Her eyes are open and it looks as if small patches of grey paper on her eyeballs.
"I honestly don't think I can adequately make a conclusion to that. I was safely tuck in the comfort of my home when all this happened. Save for a few people who I hear fled, I was surrounded by folk from my tribe hence less trouble." I say. A sense of guilt strikes through my spine. I realize that wasn't the best answer I could have provided.
She taps the space beside her on the mat as she interchanges her tin cup to the left hand.
"Now, will you stop squatting there like a mother duck and sit here."
I sit. I wonder how she knows I am squatting. A man drops a ten-shilling coin in the cup.
"God bless you." She tells him as he walks away.
"It was on a Sunday morning when the results had been announced. Our man had won and their man hadn't. That's what it's all about. Whose man is whose. Unfortunately, we were on their land. A crowd of their boys brandishing machetes ran up the rough road towards our compound. My husband beckoned me to go inside with our twin daughters and close the door. As he closed the gate, I didn't know. It was the second and last time I would see him shed a tear. The first one was at the altar, for love. This was fear."

She starts shedding tears and I make as if to reach for my handkerchief but she raises her arm in protest,
"Do not! I told you not to pity."
I wonder how she notices what I was doing.
The church pastor passes by and drops a coin. He opens his mouth as if to speak but changes his mind and walks away. She doesn't tell him "God bless " like she does to the others.
"I sent him to talk to God and his answers didn't auger well with me. I might have uttered a few insults." She tells me as if she knew I would ask.
"They brought down our gate chanting that they were cleansing their land of bad blood. That there would be no peace if their man isn't in power. I peeked through a crack on the window and saw how they hacked my husband. He squirmed a little and then lay motionless. They raised their weapons to the sky and shouted the name of their man saying he should live long.
She wipes her tears with her left palm.
"I can come another day we finish." I say.
She turns and faces me but doesn't respond. I face away as if she can see through me.
"They raped us. I closed my eyes not to see what they did to my small twin daughters. I closed my eyes to escape the moment. I found momentary peace thinking about a life without troubles. I closed my eyes and imagined my twins as princesses who did not know this situation. Little queens whose loud cries I did not hear."
"You should have stayed in your place with your people!" They shouted when they walked out.
“My twin daughters died in hospital. They say they succumbed to death but that is just a soft term. It doesn't make me forgive them; them who killed my babies. They died and my eyes could not see. I had my eyes sprayed with some strange chemical that doctors would not tell me. But would it help if I knew? No. No it would not I am still blind.
That is why sometimes I wish I could come inside church and speak to God. I am bitter that he created tribes instead of a nation with the same tribe of people. So that we won't fight. So that we all become blind, blind towards our differences."
There is silence for a minute.
"Help me up and hand me my staff. The last person has come out of church.”
That night my thoughts are swayed. Of whether there has been peace since the last electioneering period. I remember how our man and theirs greeted each other on national television to end the fight in the country. They laughed in the face of many who had fought for their course. I concluded this; Peace doesn't see differences of tribe or race. Peace is very, very blind.
