[nectar_dropcap color="#0a0a0a"]W [/nectar_dropcap]hen you are leaving Gusiiland for the capital, there are essentials which you cannot afford to leave without. If you attempt to sneak out without them, ancestors could send a delegation of witches that would slash your manhood so that you always have to sit when you are taking a piss. Being that I love these your urinals, I could not risk a life where I can’t be able to shower those colorful and nice smelling balls with my excrete juices. I’m talking about the balls in the urinal bawl, not the…never mind.

You need to carry your accent like a badge of honor as you travel. Tell the driver, “<em>Gwacha kidogo nikochoe</em>”, when you get to Narok. Even if you do not have the accent, fake it. Terrorize your fellow travelers with questions that are laced with too much accent they are left to ponder whether you are speaking in tongues. Make a phone call when there is silence in the and ask in the characteristic Kisii high pitch, “<em>Ile pag irikuwa na masigwa mrikuumbuka mkanigwekea</em>?” After the call, hold a brief monologue, “<em>mama yiangu anakuwanga na mcheso sana, timanyeti ng’aki akora</em>.” You have to finish off with a little Kisii so that it sounds like a spell. When everyone is dozing off, shout out of the blues, “<em>Gaki nyasaye one okare igoro!</em>” Sit back and watch their horrified faces as they hold on to their seats expecting the bus to float or something and send short prayers to their gods for journey mercies.

Your vast knowledge in many a field should be unleashed forth for consultation even when you know nothing more than John Snow. Remember, you are an expert in everything. The wisdom you have acquired should not be left to wash out in vain. If your bus is stopped by traffic officers, rise to the occasion. <em>“Unachua hawa Porisi mimi hawakuwai niwesa. 2004 nilikuwa naendesa canter kutoka Puusia kwenda Nairobi. Wakinisimamisa nawaambia kama wao ni wanaume wanifuate. Hawatakangi kupembeleswa</em>.”
<blockquote>
<h3>Pretend like you know everything until you believe it yourself; Look how well it worked for Nyakundi.</h3>
</blockquote>
Even when it is something as mundane as mahindi choma at Suswa, say how these Kalenjins don’t know how to roast it. Inform whoever is sitting next to you how good you are at roasting maize that if they tasted it they would become vegetarians forever. When you get to Sotik, make them wince as you over-zealously disturb the quiet confines of the bus with a fabricated narration on how you once chased Kipsigis warriors through the night towards Kericho when they attempted to steal cattle from your village. Be. The. Sensei!

As you were in Gusiiland, always carry with you your knack for unnecessary details. Passing by the University of Nairobi, relate how the senior lecturer in the Veterinary Sciences is a village-mate of yours. Describe with aptness how simple he is although he earns a load of cash. At the Central Bank, say you have a nephew who is a senior manager. Tell your seatmate how you can withdraw money without your ID because you know who is who in there. In fact, go ahead and prove it by making a phone call to this said nephew whom you have saved as, ‘<em>Nephew Central Bank’</em>.
<blockquote>
<h3>Of course, he will not pick up because that particular person is not even your nephew. The closest thing you share with them is the village borehole.</h3>
</blockquote>
But because you think everyone else has a yoghurt for a brain, you’ll still beat stories about him. “He is probably buy handling ministers you know, this is where they bank,” Say this to clear any doubts.

But you are not a Gusii person if you are not kind. Generosity will ooze from you as you travel as you travel. This is why despite your perennial stupidity and poor sense of fashion (I mean, sport shoes and a full suit really??), the gods of your ancestors will grant you forgiveness. At a lunch stop, you will invite your seatmate if they cannot afford it. If you alight with two sacks of matoke and see some other Kosto moron strut their buttocks away in a sling bag talking about “<em>naenda zangu kula biriani” </em>or some shit like that, you will offer to give them a sack because you don’t understand why they want to fast. Your kindness rises above you like a flag.

So, if you happen to see me in a bus ride from Kisii to the city, always remember one thing; I am Kisii before I am your friend. I was born at the foothill of Sameta Hill in the village of Bondonya. I am in the lineage of the great Sakawa, who was a graduate of the Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry. I am a Kisii through and through. If I put up an act, please do not bestow on me the pressure of friendship, I shall switch to it when we get to <em>Nyairobi.</em>
