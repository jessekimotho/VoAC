I have read numerous books and watched a number of movies which sought to narrate the genocide that took place in Rwanda in 1994 that left slightly more than a million Tutsis dead. <em>Left To Tell</em>, a personal account by Emily Illibagaeza left me close to tears. By the time we finished the movie <em>Hotel Rwanda</em> by Paul Rusesabagina, the entire hall was jerking tears. However, reading <strong><em>We wish to inform you that tomorrow we shall be killed with our children</em> </strong>was entirely a new and fresh experience, a kind of<em> vuja de.</em>

In this book, the author takes a multi-dimensional look at the entire Rwandan genocide. He narrates the history of the breeding hate between the two polarized groups, the Hutus and Tutsis, tracing it back to the Hamitic theory that was coined by John Speke, a theory that claimed that the long-nosed Tutsis and Ethiopians are the original ancestors of Africa thus placing them at the top of the social tier. The theory is perpetuated by the Belgian colonists when they put the Tutsis in power during the colonial period, aggravating the Hutus even more. When the Hutus rise to power in their fight for independence, they continue in their hatred and seek to exterminate the Tutsis completely out of the sheer belief that the Tutsis, as long as they are alive, would pose a threat to them.

The author goes further and analyzes the masterminds behind the massacre. He argues that the basic organization of the Hutus into interhamwe, was just a surface level organization to show that the massacre had been thoroughly planned and that though the international community had received warnings prior to the massacre that saw so many people killed within a span of three months, they did nothing to quell the lasting hatred.

We are also shown the reaction of the African community at that time, how the states within the region took polarized positions with regard to the situation. Congo, under Mobutu Sese Seko, took full support for the Hutu killings and provided a perfect avenue where the French could supply arsenal to the Hutus; arsenal that was later shipped into Rwanda to facilitate the killing. Countries like Uganda on the other hand, though did not intervene in the massacre, join hands and fight alongside the FAR, the army that liberated Rwanda from the Hutu oppression and together out Mobutu, bringing Kabila into power.

The book has essentially interwoven the various factors that came into play before, during and after the massacre. This is a literary piece that is a combination of history and emotion. Reading this book left a number of questions in my mind. The silence of the international community was more of a call for Africa, as a continent to stand up and defend itself. We have, for long been dependent on the international intervention, which is not always best, as evidenced by how the international community handled the prosecution of the perpetrators of the massacre. Even after the genocide, Rwanda was left on the brink of teetering in abject poverty, but all through, had risen from the ashes of the war, which is something I believe the whole of Africa should look up to.

If you are a lover of history, whether or not you have had enough about the Rwandan massacre, I believe this book is worth your read. This is a volume of pages that will take you through an emotional rollercoaster, making you question the where the humane humanity is, but close with restoring your faith in humanity, as you look at how the Tutsis though struggling with pain, strove to forgive the Hutus and work hand in hand with them towards building a better country.
