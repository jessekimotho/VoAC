<strong>The Kite Runner- Khaled Hosseini.</strong>

I had to read this book twice, and yet each time was as refreshing as the first time. One thing I really loved about this book, and about Hosseini’s writing in general (His two other books) is how he plays around with simple English words to come up with really creative sentences, almost like Paul Kalanithi. Reading this book, I felt that every word mattered, that if one sentence was removed from this 340 paged book, the story wouldn’t have made sense. Hosseini’s writing is ridden with pure emotion. The relationship between the characters is pristine, even if flawed, which makes the writing even more riveting.

The Kite Runner is a fictional narration that stretches over a span of more than twenty-five years. The overarching themes presented in this literary text include friendship, guilt, war and tribal oppression. Hosseini begins the book by painting the strong bond of friendship between Amir, a Pashtun, and Hassan, a Hazara. In Afghanistan, the Hazaras are treated as slaves to the Pashtuns for the longest time in history. By creating a sense of camaraderie between a Pashtun and a Hazara, Hosseini presents the main conflict in the story- the indecisiveness of a Pashtun boy on whether to bask in the glory of his tribe or stand by his friendship with a Hazara.

It would be easy for Amir to recoil and enjoy the privileges of being Pashtun were it not for the circumstantial factors that led to the friendship between him and Hassan. Amir’s father, Baba, shares a strong friendship with Hassan’s father, Ali, despite them being from tribes that have master-slave relations, having grown up together. They treat each other as brothers. Amir and Hassan grow up together too, and even feed off the same breast- that of a Hazara woman- as Amir’s mother dies during his birth and Hassan’s mother elope immediately after he’s born. The two children spend quality time together, with Amir reading Hassan stories under their favorite pomegranate tree as Hassan is illiterate.

In their friendship, more than once does Hassan stand up for Amir. When Assef, a rich Pashtun wants to hit Amir with brass knuckles, it is Hassan who steps in, pointing his slingshot at Assef, and warning his that he would destroy his eye. During the kite-running, Kabul’s favorite children’s game, Hassan helps Amir fly his kite and eventually, win. It is Hassan who runs after the last fallen kite, and even when cornered by Assef and his friends, he refuses to hand the kite over. He lets Assef sodomize him, as long as he delivers the kite to Amir (a very grotesque narration). Amir witnesses Hassan being raped, but out of fear, runs away crying. Later, Hassan comes home bleeding, and hands the kite over to Amir, who chooses to ignore the guilt simmering inside him. He takes the kite to his father, hoping that would win the affection and attention he has been fighting for ever since he was born. Since then, even though Amir receives the affection from his father (which is short-lived), he is forced to live with the guilt of not standing up for Amir.

War breaks out in Afghanistan, and both Amir and his Baba leave the country for America under very strenuous circumstances. In America, Amir studies, becomes a writer and marries. Later, after his father dies, he receives a call from Rahim Khan, his childhood friend, asking him to go to Afghanistan. ‘There is a way to be good again,’ Rahim Khan says. Amir flies back to be told that Hassan died and left behind a son, Sohrab. Rahim also informs him that Hassan wasn’t Ali’s son, but Baba’s thus, his half-brother. Hosseini later narrates of the struggles Amir faces as he tries to rescue Sohrab.

Reading this book, I was left wondering why people do good deeds. Baba builds orphanages, helps the poor, and even risks his life in some instances to save other people supposedly out of guilt. It is the guilt that he brought forth an illegitimate child, Hassan, born outside the wedlock that compels him to be kind. Baba and Amir, his son, are in many ways similar as guilt drives the father as much as the son. Amir risks death too, in trying to save Sohrab, arguably out of guilt that he didn’t stand for Hassan, his half-brother while he was raped. Is it possible that guilt can be channeled properly to make the best of us as human beings? Hosseini paints Baba as a non-religious person, simply driven by guilt to do good to other people. What are the chances, therefore, of being moral, without being religious?
