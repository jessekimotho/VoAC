Lord of the Flies, Chapter Nine [A View to a Death]

&nbsp;

&nbsp;

By the time I got to the tenth page of this book, I had already conceded that I was interacting with the work of a literary genius. Golding’s writing style is surprisingly hypnotizing. His diction is plain and simple yet captivating. His narration draws you in slowly but surely, and before you know it, you are hooked! It’s like falling in love with your best (or childhood) friend- you never know when exactly it happens. The bottomline is this - Golding’s storyline is straightforward, progressive even, which made it really easy for me to follow his train of thought. Besides, there are few characters who he repeatedly brings up, taking his ample time to build them from scratch such that by the time you are done reading, you must be attached to either one or two of them, if not all. Additionally, it helps that this book is set in a particular geographic location, a desolate island. All the events unfolding revolve around this place which, simplifying it further for the reader.

&nbsp;

<em>‘Lord of the Flies</em>’ is an ingenious representation of the dark an uncivilized nature of man once placed in the ‘proper’ conditions. The book presents the detrimental repercussions such incivility could have to the world. In this book, Golding narrates of a plane full of British schoolboys being evacuated from a raging war in their hometown. The plane is shot down, and the boys find themselves in a forlorn island and with no adult present to guide them through the daily rigors of life. As one flips through the pages, Golding shows how the boys progress from the ‘civilized’ British life they had been brought up in to a degenerate savage way of thinking. In the beginning, the boys enjoy the life of freedom in the absence of the adults. They lose their clothes and paint their faces with mud. Most of their days are spent chanting in circles and chasing after pigs which they kill, roast and eat while they wait for the ‘adults’ to come and save them. However, over time, fear, hatred and division creep in - fear that there is a demon perched on top of the island’s hill, hatred and division stemming from the fight for power between Ralph and Jack.

&nbsp;

The book explores various themes ranging from leadership. the incessant human struggle for power to the loss of innocence. One theme that cuts across the numerous pages is the human struggle between civilization and savagery. Golding’s insinuation is that civilization has the power to tame man’s savage nature, and thus maintain a sense of order within the society. This is seen in the book when the fire (the children’s closest connection to civilization) goes off and the entire island descends into anarchy. For example, it is after the fire goes off that Piggy dies after a rolling stone is cast at him. At the same time, extreme and unmonitored civilization is bound to eradicate certain members of the society, just as when one of the children is consumed by the forest fire.

&nbsp;

It’s easy to note that in this book, the entire group of children on the island is composed of a bunch of boys, a factor that stirred unrest among the feminists around the world as they felt underrepresented. However, when confronted with this question in an interview, Golding revealed the rationale behind this decision. By creating an island full of boys alone, he was effectively able to focus on the fable/ allegorical development rather than on the intricate and sometimes romantic relationships that might have come up if boys and girls had to interact in such a closed-up setting. It is in this interview that Golding was quoted saying that women have much more power than men, and that it would be quite fallacious for them to try proving that they are equal to men when they are more than that.

&nbsp;

Bearing in mind that this book was published in 1954, nine years after the devastating second world war that left more than two million (a quarter) of Belarus’ population dead, and the world’s economy on her knees, I am inclined to believe that William Golding was communicating a message to the world - the need for civilization. There was, and still is, need for civilization if we as a human race are meant to survive. To me, this fable communicates the message that it is of utmost importance for we, humans, to proceed with moderation in all our undertakings if we wish to see a better life for our progeny.

&nbsp;

With such a crucial message, it’s quite easy to see how Golding ended up winning the Nobel Prize for Literature (of course, there definitely were other reasons). That Golding chose to use a fable, and a captivating one at that, to deliver such a mundane yet important message makes this book fit for whichever audience. ‘Lord of the Flies’ is a book that can be read for enjoyment due to its catchy prose. A deeper analysis accompanied with quiet moments of rumination would later reveal to the reader the power that such a work of art is bound to have to its audience on a global level.
