So, I was just right about now getting cosy in my digz, binge-watching The Hobbit since I am on a break from the drudgery that is Kenyan campus life, when it hit me that today is the last day of November. Through the year I have maintained a streak of writing a post every month in these streets for you folks, and it would be a shame if I did not aspire to maintain that. As summer bunny Solomon Jesse (Resident book reviewer) would say, “dedication over motivation”. Maybe someday when you all decide to pay me for beating stories in this space, I will seek to write weekly. Haisuru, I will lighten up my travel category this time with a somewhat detailed tale of my recent short sojourn at the Republic of South Africa.

Acquiring a stamp of approval in the name of a Visa from the South African High Commission is a very daunting and extremely draining process, or at least that’s what it proved to be for me. In a continent where we are all referred to as a shithole, we ought to be tolerant to each other with matters immigration. Kenya offers all South Africans Visas on arrival though South Africa requires that all Kenyans make an application that takes at least a week to be reviewed before issuance (or rejection thereof). I don’t see the logic. Or maybe there are levels to this thing. In this continent, we are all equal shitholes, but it seems there are those who are more equal than the rest. They do not accept just anything that is dumped in them. Na-ah. They are those ones that require about three flashes for shit from other parts to go through. They are like those toilets where if it’s a bad day for you, your guava filled defecation will not flow with the water. It will float and stare back at you, leaving you in a state of panic as on the other side of the door stands your girlfriend’s dad. It’s just a bad day.

And a bad day it was for me. Five thousand Kenyan Shillings, a Yellow fever vaccination, a completed application form in black ink, the feathers of a dodo, confirmed flight bookings, the blood of a cow with green tits and valid bank statements is all I required to get things going. Sounds easy. Since I was to travel with the galdem, I decided we should all make our Visa application at the same time and synchronize the process. Her passport was not yet out from Nyayo house, and that’s a whole other blogpost of frustrations that I will pen another time. When her passport was finally processed, we barely had a week to make our application in time for our travel arrangements. This triggered the hustle for gathering documentation. Neither of us have bank statements with sufficient funds hence we had to move around trying to convince our paranoid African parents that their statements were not being offered to the Illuminati. (I wonder what exactly they mean sufficient funds, because I have been able to survive in this pad for the past week with ugali nyanya and the strength provided by the blood of the lamb).

The first day submitting our application saw us waste valuable Kenyan currency in fare. We were turned back and told to top our documents with confirmed hotel bookings for all the days we intended to stay in the South. “Ooh, and darlings, you do not need those hideous bank statements because everything is catered for you by your South African host.” Hallelujah praise the Lord.

We discard the bank statements, whoosh home, request our hotel bookings be sent over and then wake up the next day all the way from the furthest corners of Thika road to Westlands and make our application again. “The Visa is processed between 5-8 business days...,” so reads their website. We are here, on a Friday, exactly five business days to our projected travel date and we can only request our ancestors to expedite this process.

During this period, I am juggling between CATs, lab practicals and my Drive show on radio. My body is about to burst from all these responsibilities. As we walk out of the application centre with our receipts waiting eagerly for the collection day, we chant “It is well”.

It wasn’t. On the eve of collection, I receive a text I consider malicious, full of typos, requesting me to avail my tired Kenyan bottoms to the South African High Commission all the way in Lenana Road to submit bank statements. These guys are nut jobs. First you tell me you need them, then you say you don’t, now you need them? Sounds like the relationship between me and Kenyan education. I am pissed. Naturally, since our applications were similar, I expected that the galdem had received the texts also. Turns out I was wrong. We speculated that either they were to text her the next day, or somehow her Meru ancestors had worked overtime for her application to go through, while that old geezer Sakawa was playing darts with my other Kisii ancestors.

So, I am at Lenana Road, the gateman is Kisii, and he chats me up asking why I am there. I show him the text and he request to see the bank statements before he gives me a pass. I say I don’t have them, and he goes, “No, Shit?!” Okay, actually he doesn’t but it would have been awesome if he did. On second thought, that would sound really horrible in Kisii. Anyways, I tell him I don’t need bank statements and I am here to explain the same. I leave my phone at the gate with galdem because I might be trying to hack the High Commission with my cheap android and discover the cause of xenophobic attacks in their country. Hey, I’m kidding. Embassies and commissions world over require that phones be left at the gate so I’m down with that.

I’m going to paint a picture of the South African High Commission so that you guys can see my ordeal clearly. Okay, here we go. The offices are on the second floor of the building, there is a guard at the lift on the ground floor who requests to see your gate pass before allowing you to proceed. On alighting the second floor is another guard who then escorts or points you to the room. When I get here, there is a lady and man behind a glass screen (Like those at banking halls) and I immediately realise they are Kenyan. A sigh of relief as I stand up to be served, hoping my fellow Kenyans will help me out. Boy, was I wrong!

“Haro, how may I heop you?”
“ Okay, I am to travel tomorrow to South Africa but I received a text to submit bank statements first”
“And where are they”
“Hehe, thing is, I was told at the application center that you don’t need them”
“Okay, then I am telling you that we need them”
“ Who is your host”
“ A European company that has offices in South Africa”
“ Then tell them to submit their statements or you submit yours”
“ Thing is, I do not have sufficient bank statements personally, and I do not think they can give me their bank statements.”
“ Then I cannot heop you”

Help goddamit, just say Help.

I was getting agitated at his insistence on being adamant and I could see he clearly was getting agitated. So, I walk out, back to the gate and make a phone call to my hosts. They insist that they are a big corporation with roots allover more than 10 countries and it would be virtually impossible to trust a young man from the unknown Sameta hill in Kisii county with their bank statements. Its like telling Coca-Cola or Safaricom to provide someone they have never met with bank statements. So, I request them to call the High Commission before I am told again to go hug a porcupine. They say fine. I head back inside and the ninja looks confused to sight my big head in the premises.

“Wewe si nimekuambia utoke hapa?”
“Hapana, it’s not like that, ni vile you don’t want to understand my situation”
“ Tell the host to send their bank statement or you bring yours as an employee of your company”

“First of all, my company is a startup, I don’t have a pay slip. Secondly, my hosts have been trying to call you guys, but you are not picking their calls”

“We are not picking any calls boss. Leave this premises or risk rejection for your Visa.”

I get angry. This is 8th November, my travel date is the morning of 9th and the dude doesn’t seem like he gave a rat’s ass about my arrangements. In my desperation, I lodge a heated argument with him. Him shouting behind the glass, me shouting on the other side. Before I know it, the guard manning the floor’s lift taps my shoulder.

“Boss, toa huyo mtu hapa!!”

The guy yells angrily behind the glass as I am escorted out like a disorderly drunk. The other citizens sited on the waiting benches stare at me, a misplaced agitated 20year old in their eyes, and I feel like barking at their faces. Indeed, men are dogs if given a chance.

I am literally given a shove into the elevator by the guard and as it descends to the ground floor, I look up and see my South African dream rise up in smoke.
