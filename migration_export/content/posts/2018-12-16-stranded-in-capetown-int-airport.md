<p>“Do you guys know which terminal you are supposed to go to?” Queries my dad from the driver’s seat as we approach the security check point off Mombasa road getting to JKIA.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>“What’s a terminal?” I ask naively.<br />He chuckles softly, turns to look at me and wipes the grin after he realizes that I am serious.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><br />The closest I had been to an airport to this point is back in my form two at Entebbe in Uganda. My old man had just been thoroughly disappointed after expecting a fat cheque from work and having already pre-planned a flight for us back to Kenya at the end of our short holidaying period in the buzzling city of Kampala. He decided to take us to the airport and point to us like he was God and were Moses, “There is the promised flight, you can see it but you shall never board it”</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>It was therefore somewhat of an exciting moment that the person seeing me and the galdem off on both of our first flights was the man who first showed me the entrails of an airport.<br />“I’m very sure it’s somewhere here in the boarding pass.” I chyme as I check my mail for the specifics, in time to alighting from the car for the security screening.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>xxxxxxxxxx<br />The last time I left you burgers, I had no hopes of getting me a Visa after being kicked out of the South African High Commission<a href="http://www.voiceofanafricanchild.com/thrown-out-of-south-africas-high-commision/"> here.</a> After that torturous purgatory-like ordeal, I walk out of their gates muttering and cursing under my breath, wishing that the fool is struck by bouts of unending diarrhea or someone laces the tissue paper he uses with pepper. The Kisii guard at the gate requests me for a handout and in my frustrated state, I really care less about money. I whip out a crisp note and give it to him, walk out and order a cab. Me and the galdem head out to the Visa application center at Westlands to check whether her Visa is out.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Her Visa was out fam. Same documentation with mine, but she got hers and I hadn’t. No bank statements but she got hers and I hadn’t. It is the evening of November 8th, we are to travel together on the morning of 9th, she got hers and I hadn’t. I phone my South African host company and update them of the events and they promise to send in their bank statements because they really had no other option. Plus, there was no way I was going to miss my first time on a flight, I’d freaking walk all the way to Zimbabwe and take a flight from their if so be it. Inevitably, we had to reschedule the flight to the next week because there was no way those burgers were going to receive the bank statements and hand me my Visa on the same day. Haisuru. Heading out to Waiyaki way to catch a mat home, a plane passes just right above Safaricom House, I clench my teeth and raise my fist up and shake it in anger. I feel like the gods are having a laugh at my miserable ass. I ask galdem what she thinks we shall do if my Visa doesn’t come out at all.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><br />"Maybe you should go solo and represent us well." I say. <br />"If yours isn’t processed I won’t go either, there’s no point." She says back. <br />     Love is foolish you guys.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>      xxxxxxxx</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><br />Monday 12th November, My Visa is out, and I am rushing around like an adolescent on Viagra. I head to pick it from Westie, pick a suitcase from Kiambu, book our flights for the next morning of 13th, get excited, see my barber all the way in Kangundo road for a decent cut. For some reason, the flight bookings were not going through on any airline that night. 3am and we didn’t have a booking yet, the credit card kept getting rejected. Swinging between excitement and anxiety, my feelings were allover the place. We were so close, yet so far. It was as late as 5am when the booking went through. I was unable to catch sleep for the remaining time, our flight was scheduled for the afternoon and I couldn’t believe it that the evening of the same day I would be in Johannesburg ready for my connecting flight to Capetown. Yaani me, the grandson of Chang’aa sellers in the village of Bondonya at the foot of Sameta Hill. Somebody slap the fuck out of me so I can wake up from this absolute dream.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>I phone my old man and convince him to drop us off because Matiang’i had scared matatus off their job. I estimated cab fare from Thika road all the way to JKIA could be enough to open a small kindergarten for all the kids named Jayden in my neighborhood. We chill at the Java House in JKIA before time, having absolutely no idea that there is a difference between boarding time and departure time and almost missed boarding as we were the last ones in. We took our sweet time licking the sauce off our plates with the costly Java fries. (It is at this point I should mention that the attendants at JKIA Java are lazy, extremely racist Kenyans, and just purely arrogant. But this is a blogpost for another time also.)- Or they just didn’t like my big head, you never know with its weird shape the types of emotions it elicits.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><br />Take-off is extremely amazing. His verses in the Migos songs always hit the spot. Ooh, it’s the flight. Sorry. I meant, the plane taking off is an out of this world feeling because well, you’re being lifted off the surface of the world. As the engine roared and the wings heaved, I could not believe what was about to happen. Something extremely monumental and historic in my life, I could finally get all those memes about flying from that day onwards! Early on, I had passed the fat blokes at the business class, all of them with rich smirks and probably going down south to see ‘why the office in Gauteng was not churning the right numbers.’ I looked at them, with such huge leg room , a very comfortable seat with better leaning capabilities than the economy class and them big-ass headphones. Lucky bastards! I didn’t care much though, I wanted to be on a plane and I was. The world from way up looks so insignificant, everything looks like a dot on a piece of paper, whether it be a very big city or a huge ego. I imagined there are people having lotsa pressure with their lives, frustrations, sadness because some douchebag decided to wrong them, tribalism because they think they are better than the next man who only just speaks a different language. I looked up from my window into the heavens, saw the vast expanse of the universe and realized how the things we think are too important are very trivial to the structure of the universe we live in. After the deep thoughts I decide to take a shit on the moving plane at that altitude because that’s a cloud nine experience. Haha.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>O.R Tambo International Airport in Johannesburg is the biggest and busiest airport you can be at on this side of the planet. It’s toilets are extremely clean and they have tissue paper for days. Landing at around 6:20pm and without something to do till around 8pm when boarding to Cape town, I decided to check on my folks on WhatsApp. There was no open Wi-Fi so I decided to roam with the 50MB that I had on my Saf line. Don’t try it ever fam. Only one text managed to come through. Dear Customer, your data balance is below 2MB. My host for the first night at the University of Capetown had informed me that there would be Wi-Fi at Cape Town International Airport and so I stayed calm till then, when I’d be able to contact him for directions and also inform people I had arrived.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Finally landing at Cape town, we are happy and thankful. Armed with some thousands of Kenyan shillings in cash and some more on my debit card, we headed out to look for an exchange bureau in the airport. <em>Ha</em>, <em>Shaka</em>.11pm at Cape town International Airport, no exchange bureau is open, and we don’t have a cent in South African Rand. I say it’s okay. We can always change the next day. So I fish out my phone to text my host on and inform him of my arrival so they can facilitate my taxi until I can be able to change my currency.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><br />No open Wi-Fi network. Wow, Okay, don’t panic. Not much people at the airport at this point but don’t panic. No way to know how you’re supposed to call your host or even inform them of your predicament, but don’t panic.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Two 20-year-olds, a useless Kenyan sim-card, no Wi-Fi, Useless Kenyan currency at this point and completely clueless on how to proceed. Ayam tired and stranded in a foreign country. I sigh, sit down and close my eyes. I flashback to my ordeal at the High Commission.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>You cannot cheat death twice.</p>
