<p>There is a vast difference between waking up in the noisy, smoke ridden, traffic plagued, hawker heckling, <em>mare mare</em> neighbourhoods of Nairobi and the ambience of the cool, warm, sunny morning – filled with the humming of exotic birds – that I woke up to in my room at the University of Cape Town (UCT).</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><strong>You wake up at the crack of dawn at Cape Town whereas you survive the night in Nairobi. </strong></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The cocks that usually crow in the morning at my place sound exhausted and I am sure they are plotting to abdicate their role and let the goats take over giving the morning shout-outs like a certain Deejay Khalid.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>I am not saying this out of spite or disrespect for my own hometown. I think you would pen the same description if you woke up, drew your curtains and were faced with the Table Mountains, a wonder of the world. Campus shuttles plodded the streets below, picking up students and dropping them off. Uber drivers roamed about waiting for those requiring extra comfort to access their classes. <em>Just wow</em>. That is what I call arrogant privilege. I imagined myself riding an uber in the streets of Kenyatta University heading to sit for my retake. <br /><br />Wild, fam.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:separator --><hr class="wp-block-separator" /><!-- /wp:separator -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><em>Previously on Survivor…</em></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Last time we met, Galdems and I was <a href="http://www.voiceofanafricanchild.com/stranded-in-capetown-int-airport/">stranded at Cape Town International Airport</a> with no means whatsoever of getting to UCT. After a few minutes of meditation, I remembered I had <em>kitu</em> a hundred shillings in my Mpesa. I buy airtime, call my host and quickly inform him of my predicament because the roaming rates wouldn’t allow for longer talks. A wise man, he orders a cab, calls me back with the plate numbers of our driver and his name. As for currency exchange, the hosts were travelling to Kenya sooner than us and therefore needed the Kenyan cash we had and we their Rands. Easy work. <br /><br />Relieved, we step out of the airport and head to our pick-up point. The first thing I do is deeply inhale the Cape Town air and I was immediately shook to find no smell of mutura in the atmosphere. Such arrogant sophistication. I thought these guys were big on diversity? <em>Henwei</em>.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>It is either Kenyan taxi drivers have utter disregard for the navigation tools of their apps or we are years behind Cape Town drivers in discipline. There are no calls with questions like “<em>Uko wapi? </em>Eeh? <em>Sikupati vizuri</em>…ati Nation Centre?...g<em>ani?</em>...Si usimame side ya KFC ninatoka na Kimathi street.” There is no “karibu boss, unaenda wapi?” Because in Capetown, the maps make sense. Just sit, connect your bluetooth, jam to Mpongo Love as the cab cuts across the first-world highways and the navigator guides the driver to your desired destination.. I thought, “damn I could just throw away my passport and live here”. This driver dude even stopped at a red light when it was midnight and zero cars on the roads.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>If he was in Kenya,<em> catch him like a rat on the haiweei.</em></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The population in Cape Town is predominantly white, or at least it’s what my quack analytics picked up. (I am not aware of the factual figures but I strongly suspect Quack Analytica was pretty on point). <em>Haidhuru</em>. The roads are extremely clean, I bet the germs in their streets even say the grace and wash their hands before feasting. Fancy glass houses, well-manicured lawns, balcony swimming pools and electric gates were sending shock waves through my village consciousness. Tour buses, cable cars to the mountain tops, efficient shuttle services and beach houses with helipads? <em>Did we just get to Canaan before my mans Rao?</em></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>We had our first experience at McDonalds, which is just another KFC with a fancy name. We however still ate to our fill, which we indeed ought to have because our first cold dish of racism was boiling in the kitchen.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>On that second day, we went trotting up Newlands Forest in Table Mountain National park with our UCT hosts. We took strolls into places even they had not been because what’s more fun than getting lost in a forest, right? I remembered what I had seen happen in the <em>‘Wrong Turn’</em> movies and decided to put on some music to ease tension. Some white dudes were jogging and cycling (who the hell cycles up a mountain?) or walking dogs up the forest. Most of the dogs were as big as calves, looked very mature and composed and walked with a springy gait that suggested they ate three course meals and had butlers with names like ‘Clifford’ or Alfred’.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>As we hiked, we also did a little dancing to the music beaming from my phone. Two of the dogs felt like they were being provoked and started barking. All of us panicked, like the Africans we are, and started running. Those beasts decide they like black meat, open their mouths wide and start chasing.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p> <br />            “Stop <em>fucking</em> running!!” shouted the white dog-owner.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><br />I stop running because the barking is a bit far off, turn back and realize galdem is being chased in circles. She is still clutching to her handbag though, despite her screams and calls for help as she hopelessly tries to outrun the enraged canines. What exactly do women carry in those bags that they have to hold on tightly to? I guessed it could be printed screenshots to be unleashed for when they are needed. <br /><br />“I said stop running, what the <em>fuck</em> is wrong with you?” the white dude shouted again at which point we realized he was directing that to us and not to his dogs.<br /><br />Wow.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>All along, this guy had been hurling insults and ranking us lower than his animals. All along, us acting on our gut instincts, running away from his dogs and reacting to adrenaline was <em>fucking</em> wrong, more than his dogs randomly going after us.<br /><br />The rest of us were now standing at a safe distance, attention shifted to her and her bag of screenshots, like sunflower to the sun. When she finally stopped, she was exhausted, scared to the bone and angry to the core. But ironically not angrier than the dog owner who should have in the first place adhered to the rules of the National park and put his dogs on a leash. <em>Or would he have preferred having us on a leash?</em></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>            “Don’t be <em>fucking</em> idiots !”</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The dude sized us and hissed like the colonialist he was before he walked off. At that point, I wished Dedan Kimathi was hibernating somewhere in the forest with the Mau Mau crew so he’d come and psyche me. I was angered at that ‘subtle’ show of racism from someone who was obviously the one on the wrong side of the law. I ashamedly brushed it off because we were still lost in the forest and had to find our way out in time for us to move from UCT to our hotel in Century City. <br /><br />Yezzzz. A hotel. In Century City. A place on the other side of the <em>Teibal</em> Mountains (Thats how us South <em>Efricans</em> say it) where we were to participate in a tech startup/investor conference dubbed AfricArena on behalf of our startup company, Medbit</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:separator --><hr class="wp-block-separator" /><!-- /wp:separator -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>As we were heading to the hotel from our botched excursion, I looked at my galdems and then at the sun setting behind the table mountain. All that came out of my mouth was:<br /><br />“ That is the most beautiful thing I have ever seen in my life” <br /><br />The next day on a Citywide tour by our generous hosts at AfricArena, me and the galdems witnessed the sun setting on the Atlantic Ocean. <br /><br />“Screw the mountains, <em>this</em> is the most beautiful scene of my life.” <br /><br />Repeat scene the next day when I went to the Muizenberg beach.<br />And the next when we went up the top of Table mountains; magnificent view of the city tucked safely at the bottom.<br />And when I saw a Ferrari in the dealership store. <br />Or when I saw Robben island from across the ocean</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Like a four-year-old child at<em> the Show</em>.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Soon I realized almost everything I saw in Capetown was ‘more beautiful than the last’ and so I reverted from “That is the most beautiful thing I have ever seen in my life” to “Ooh shit, look at that absolute beauty.” Sounded more touristy and I didn’t have to look like a stereotypical African villager every type I opened my mouth. Cape Town changes you fam.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Our gracious hosts treated us to a dinner at the shores of the Atlantic Ocean where we had wine and chatted with big money from all over the globe. The expensive glass houses with helipads right by the beach fascinated me until it hit me that all the people I saw accessing them were white. I noted that the people serving tables at the restaurants/bars were always all black people while the manager and the guy at the counter were mostly white. <br /><br />Was this by chance or was it apartheid and institutionalized racism still playing out years after Africans had claimed independence? (<em>Yes we are talking about this.)</em></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>We were now at the last day of the conference and our hotel accommodation had automatically expired. Since we were touristy, we had intended to extend our stay in Cape Town and therefore began our search for a cheap Airbnb hosting at 6pm. There was no way we were going to afford accommodation at the same five-star hotel, located in one of the most expensive parts of Cape Town. Me and galdem sat right outside the Conference Centre where the Wi-Fi was still accessible because the halls were being closed for the evening. I still only had my Kenyan simcards like the fool I was – three days into my stay in South Africa – because we had been relying on Wi-Fi the whole time we were there. The cash I had was only an approximation of cab fare to wherever my hosting would be, where I planned to withdraw what I had in my debit card. The Math was exact fam. The guard at the conference Centre requests to know why we are still outside the halls, and we lie that our Uber is on the way as I rush to create my profile and book for accommodation. Ten minutes later, the host, a white woman, declines my request for accommodation citing that she is not around at the time.<br /><br /><em>Strange</em>. How does one have their place up open for accommodation on Airbnb and still claim not to be around? <br /><em><br />Racism</em>. <br /><br />It’s getting dangerously dark, we have no accommodation, money or food. The guard is clearly agitated at our claim to be waiting for an uber thirty minutes later. Meanwhile, I’m still looking at the profile of this white mama who has declined my request for no reason. <br /><br /><em>Racism.</em> <br /><br />I ask for directions from a guy leaving the conference, to direct us to the nearest mall where I would be able to purchase an MTN line for internet as I place another Airbnb request. At the mall, I buy my line, hungry, angry and getting broke real fast, I place another request which gets declined without reason offered by yet <em>another</em> white couple. <br /><br /><em>Racism</em>.<br /><br />My phone’s charge is getting to 5%, galdem’s phone is dead, the mall is closing up because it is super late, and we have no idea where we are going to sleep.<br /><br /><strong><em>Fam, It was just like my high-school principal David Gideon Kariuki used to say: As it was in the beginning, so it is now, and evermore shall be. </em></strong><br /><br />I sit on a bench and request the gods to come through for me.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Suddenly Cape Town was not so exciting for me. Suddenly, the allure of being in a foreign land wasn’t so exciting for us. The galdem was close to tears, a very frustrated human being. I was feeling hopeless, especially reminiscent of the fact that I had struggled to get to these lands and here I was struggling to survive the experience as well.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>My phone beeps, it’s my editor on this blog. He suggests to us that we google for the cheapest hotels and lodgings nearest to us. Reluctantly, we do so and manage to find some, in places around Cape Town whose names we couldn’t pronounce. The name Cape Town was itself beginning to taste bitter in my Kenyan mouth. I longed for Nairobi, for mahindi choma, for humans who could accommodate me in their house, for humans who don’t care about skin color. I did not play <em>Mpongo Love</em> in the cab on our way to the hotel that night.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Because at that point, Cape Town did not deserve any love from me.</p>
